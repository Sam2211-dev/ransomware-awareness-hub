Introduction to Ransomware

Understanding what ransomware is and how it affects organizations and individuals.

What is Ransomware?

Ransomware is a type of malicious software that encrypts a victim's files or locks them out of their system. The attackers then demand a ransom from the victim to restore access to the data upon payment.

Users are shown instructions for how to pay a fee to get the decryption key. The costs can range from a few hundred dollars to thousands, payable to cybercriminals in cryptocurrency such as Bitcoin.

The Scale of the Threat

Ransomware attacks have increased dramatically in recent years, affecting businesses, government agencies, healthcare organizations, and individuals. The global cost of ransomware damage is projected to exceed $20 billion annually.

Recent high-profile attacks have targeted critical infrastructure, including utilities, hospitals, and supply chains, highlighting the evolving severity of this threat.

User Education

Learn about different types of ransomware, their history, and the impact on victims.

Types of Ransomware

Ransomware comes in many variants, each with unique characteristics and approaches:

  • Crypto Ransomware: Encrypts files on your device, making them inaccessible.
  • Locker Ransomware: Locks you out of your device entirely.
  • Double Extortion: Not only encrypts your data but threatens to leak it publicly.
  • Mobile Ransomware: Specifically targets smartphones and tablets.

History of Ransomware

The first documented ransomware attack occurred in 1989 with the "AIDS Trojan." Since then, ransomware has evolved significantly, with major outbreaks including CryptoLocker (2013), WannaCry (2017), and more recent attacks like REvil and DarkSide.

Mitigation & Awareness

Essential strategies to protect yourself and your organization from ransomware attacks.

Prevention Strategies

  • Regular Backups: Maintain offline, encrypted backups of data.
  • Software Updates: Keep all systems and software up to date with security patches.
  • Email Vigilance: Be cautious of unexpected emails, especially those with attachments or links.
  • Strong Authentication: Implement multi-factor authentication wherever possible.
  • Network Segmentation: Limit access between different parts of your network.
  • Security Awareness: Train all users to recognize and report suspicious activities.

What to Do If Attacked

If you suspect you're the victim of a ransomware attack:

  1. Disconnect affected systems from the network immediately.
  2. Report the attack to local law enforcement and the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center.
  3. Consult with cybersecurity professionals before making any decisions about paying the ransom.
  4. Restore systems from clean backups if available.
